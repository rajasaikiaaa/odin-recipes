Beef Stew

A winter dinner staple, beef stew is the hearty comfort food to make when you need a bowl of stick-to-your-bones, warming goodness.

Ingredients

  • Meat
    • Beef chuck roast, cubed into 1-1.5 inch pieces
  • Vegetables
    1. Onions, chopped
    2. Carrots, peeled and cut into chunks or rounds
    3. Potatoes, cubed (baby potatoes or Yukon Gold are recommended to hold their shape better)
    4. Celery, chopped
    5. Garlic, minced
    6. Tomato paste
  • Liquids
    1. Beef broth (or stock)
    2. Red wine (a dry red like Cabernet Sauvignon or Merlot works well; optional, can be substituted with more beef broth).
    3. Water (may be used in combination with broth or as part of the liquid base).
  • Aromatics and seasonings
    1. Olive oil (or other neutral cooking oil)
    2. All-purpose flour (for thickening)
    3. Salt and black pepper, to taste
    4. Bay leaf
    5. Dried thyme
    6. Worcestershire sauce (adds depth of flavor)
  • Garnish(Optional)
    • Fresh parsley, chopped

Steps

  • Step 1: Prepare the beef
    1. Trim any excess, easily removable fat from the beef chuck roast. Cut it into 1-1.5 inch cubes.
    2. Pat the beef dry with paper towels to ensure good browning and minimize steaming.
    3. Season the beef generously with salt and pepper.
    4. You can also toss the beef in a small amount of flour (optional, but it helps thicken the sauce later).
  • Step 2: Sear the beef
    1. Heat olive oil (or other cooking oil) in the pot over medium-high heat.
    2. Brown the beef in batches to prevent overcrowding the pot, ensuring each piece gets a nice sear and crust. Overcrowding can lead to steaming instead of browning.
    3. Remove the browned beef to a plate and set aside.
  • Step 3: Saute the aromatics and vegetables
    1. Reduce the heat to medium
    2. Add the chopped onion, garlic, and celery to the pot.
    3. Cook, stirring occasionally, until the onions are softened and slightly golden, about 5 minutes.
    4. Add the tomato paste and cook for an additional minute, stirring to combine.
  • Step 4: Deglaze and add the liquids
    1. Return the browned beef and its juices to the pot.
    2. If using, add balsamic or red wine vinegar to the pot and stir, scraping up any browned bits on the bottom of the pan to add flavor to the stew base.
    3. Stir in the flour (if not done in Step 1) until it dissolves completely.
    4. Add the beef broth, red wine (if using), water, dried thyme, bay leaf, and Worcestershire sauce.
    5. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce the heat to low, ensuring a gentle simmer.
  • Step 5: Braise the stew
    1. Cover the pot with a lid.
    2. Braise in the oven at 325°F (160°C) for approximately 2 hours, or until the beef is starting to become tender.
    3. Alternatively, simmer on the stovetop over the lowest setting for the same duration, stirring occasionally to prevent sticking.
  • Step 6: Add the root vegetables
    1. After the initial braising time, add the carrots and potatoes to the pot.
    2. If cooking in the oven, return the covered pot to the oven and cook for another hour, or until the vegetables are tender and the beef is fork-tender
    3. If cooking on the stovetop, continue simmering for another 20-30 minutes, or until the vegetables are tender.
    4. Remove the bay leaf.
    5. If the sauce is not thick enough, you can simmer uncovered for 30 minutes to allow it to reduce or create a cornstarch slurry and stir it in.
  • Final adjustments and serving
    1. Taste the stew and adjust seasoning (salt and pepper) if necessary.
    2. Serve warm, optionally garnished with fresh parsley.
    3. Consider serving with crusty bread, mashed potatoes, or rice to soak up the flavorful broth.

Note: If you choose to use a slow cooker, sear the meat and saute the vegetables as instructed, then transfer everything to the slow cooker and cook on low for 7-8 hours or high for 4-5 hours. If needed, you can use a cornstarch slurry to thicken the stew at the end. Beef stew also improves in flavor when made ahead and refrigerated overnight.
